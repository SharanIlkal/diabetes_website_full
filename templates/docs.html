Documentation

1. About the project

This system predicts diabetes risk using Machine Learning. It is intended as a screening/demo tool and not a medical diagnosis. Use clinical judgement and consult healthcare professionals for medical decisions.

2. BMI

Formula: BMI = Weight (kg) / (Height in m × Height in m). Use this to estimate body mass relative to height; higher BMI can be associated with higher diabetes risk.

3. Glucose level

Categories (mg/dL):

  • Normal: 70–99 mg/dL
  • Pre-diabetes: 100–125 mg/dL
  • Diabetes: 126+ mg/dL

How glucose is measured (overview):

1) Glucometer (common for patients): A glucometer measures blood glucose from a small fingertip sample using a test strip. The strip contains enzymes (e.g., glucose oxidase or glucose dehydrogenase) — glucose reacts and produces an electrical signal which the meter converts into mg/dL. Steps: prick fingertip → apply blood to strip → read meter.

2) Laboratory blood tests: Labs perform plasma glucose measurements which are more precise. Common tests include Fasting Plasma Glucose (FPG) and Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT). Lab instruments often use reactions such as glucose oxidase–peroxidase or hexokinase methods.

4. Blood Pressure

Blood pressure categories (systolic/diastolic in mmHg). These are general clinical categories — individual targets may vary by age, pregnancy, or medical conditions.

Category Systolic (mmHg) Diastolic (mmHg) Notes
Normal 90 – 119 60 – 79 Ideal healthy range.
Elevated (Pre-high) 120 – 129 < 80 Higher than normal; lifestyle changes advised.
Stage 1 Hypertension 130 – 139 80 – 89 Medical evaluation and possible treatment recommended.
Stage 2 Hypertension ≥ 140 ≥ 90 Typically requires medical treatment.
Hypertensive Crisis ≥ 180 ≥ 120 Emergency — seek immediate medical attention.
Low Blood Pressure (Hypotension) < 90 < 60 May cause dizziness/fainting; assess symptoms & cause.
5. Family History

Family history affects diabetes risk. If close relatives (parents/siblings/grandparents) have diabetes, your risk increases due to:

  1. Genetics: Inherited traits can reduce insulin sensitivity or insulin production.
  2. Shared lifestyle: Families often share diet, activity levels, and body-weight patterns which influence risk.
  3. Risk levels: One parent with type 2 diabetes can increase risk ~2–3×; both parents raise risk further. Type 1 is also genetic but less predictable.

It’s risk, not guarantee: Having family history means higher susceptibility but does not ensure diagnosis. Preventive actions (healthy weight, activity, monitoring) can reduce risk.

6. Physical Activity

Regular physical activity helps prevent and manage diabetes by improving insulin sensitivity, lowering blood sugar, supporting weight control, and improving cardiovascular health. Simple activities — walking, cycling, or light workouts — significantly reduce diabetes risk and help maintain stable glucose levels.
